---
title: "Epanalepsis"
description: "Repetition at the end of a clause or sentence of"
---

Repetition at the end of a clause or sentence of
the word or phrase with which it began. Sidney
was using epanalepsis in writing "I might, unhappy word, O me, I might" (Astrophil and
Stella). As with symploce (q.v.), epanalepsis can
be used to evoke a sense of measured balance
between clauses: "Blood hath bought blood,
and blows have answer'd blows; Strength
match'd with strength, and power confronted
power" (Shakespeare, King John 2.1) Another
example: "Rejoice in the Lord always: and
again I say, Rejoice" (Philippians 4:4). Because
epanalepsis is apparently contrived but can still
seem to spring from intense emotion, it is more
common in poetry. When epanalepsis is used in
prose, it often creates sentences that stand alone
as aphorisms: "Nothing can be created out of
nothing" (Lucretius). "Men of few words are
the best men" (Henry IV 3.2).
