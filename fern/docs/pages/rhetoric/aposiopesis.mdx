"To become silent"; a form of ellipsis (q.v.) in
which a narrator or character either breaks off
discourse or radically changes its direction.
King Lear conveys his rage, stating, "I will have
revenge on you both that all the world shall—
I will do such things—What they are yet, I
know not; but they shall be the terrors of the
earth!" (2.4.282). Aposiopesis can be used to
express hesitation or distractedness as well as
deep emotion. The figure occurs frequently in
interior monologues where the stream of words
is not cut off so much as fragmented. Thus
Joyce writes, "All quiet on Howth now. The
distant hills seem. Where we. The rhododenin. All that old hill has seen. Names change:
thats all. lovers: yum, yum" (Ulysses 308).
Aposiopesis is sometimes difficult to distinguish
from the anacoluthon (q.v.).
