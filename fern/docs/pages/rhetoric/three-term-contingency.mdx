# The Three-Term Contingency

## Definition

The three-term contingency refers to the relationship between these three central events: the discriminative stimulus, the response, and the reinforcer.

## How It Works

A response that is emitted and reinforced in the presence of a discriminative stimulus becomes more probable in the future in the presence of the same or very similar discriminative stimulus.

## Components

### Discriminative Stimulus (Sᴰ)
An event or stimulus that precedes, and sets the occasion for, an operant behavior.

### Response (R)
The behavior emitted by the organism.

### Reinforcer (Sᴿ)
The consequence that follows the response and increases its likelihood of occurring again in the future.

## Comparison with Pavlovian Conditioning

A different relationship between stimuli, responses, and reinforcers occurs in Pavlovian conditioning. Thus, we should briefly distinguish between these two fundamental conditioning processes.

In Pavlovian conditioning, an originally neutral stimulus becomes an effective conditioned stimulus by virtue of pairings with an unconditioned stimulus. In operant conditioning, the critical relation is between the response and reinforcer: the latter makes the former subsequently more probable.

## Practical Examples

- **Lecturing**: The discriminative stimulus is a classroom with students present. The response is the professor lecturing. The reinforcer might be student engagement or learning outcomes.
- **Driving**: The discriminative stimulus is a green traffic signal. The response is driving through the intersection. The reinforcer is reaching the destination safely.

These stimuli are called discriminative stimuli because they signal which responses may lead to positive reinforcement and which may lead to the avoidance or reduction of punishment. In other words, they set the occasions on which responses may be reinforced.
