---
title: "Anacoluthon"
description: "Completing a sentence with a construction"
---

Completing a sentence with a construction
that presupposes a different beginning. Shakespeare used anacoluthon in "They who
brought me in my master's hate, I live to look
upon their tragedy" (Richard III 3.2.57). Anacoluthon is common in spoken language when
a speaker begins a sentence in a way that implies a certain logical resolution and then ends
it differently. Consequently, anacoluthon is
often found in interior monologues: "I suppose
she was pious because no man would look at
her twice I hope Ill never be like her a wonder
she didnt want us to cover our faces but she
was a welleducated woman certainly and her
gabby talk . . . " (Joyce, Ulysses).
Arthur Quinn and Lyon Rathbun
University of California, Berkeley
